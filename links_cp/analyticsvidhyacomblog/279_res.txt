<h2>Introduction</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Here&#8217;s a situation you&#8217;ve got into in your <a href="https://courses.analyticsvidhya.com/courses/introduction-to-data-science-2/?utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=6stepsnaivebayesarticle" target="_blank" rel="noopener">data science</a> project:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">You are working on a classification problem and have generated your set of hypothesis, created features and discussed the importance of variables. Within an hour, stakeholders want to see the first cut of the model.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">What will you do? You have hundreds of thousands of data points and quite a few variables in your training data set. In such situation, if I were in your place, I would have used &#8216;<strong>Naive Bayes</strong>&#8216;, which can be extremely fast relative to other <a href="https://courses.analyticsvidhya.com/courses/introduction-to-data-science-2/?utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=6stepsnaivebayesarticle" target="_blank" rel="noopener">classification algorithms</a>. It works on Bayes theorem of probability to predict the class of unknown data set.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In this article, I&#8217;ll explain the basics of this algorithm, so that next time when you come across large data sets, you can bring this algorithm to action. In addition, if you are a <a href="https://courses.analyticsvidhya.com/courses/introduction-to-data-science-2/?utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=6stepsnaivebayesarticle" target="_blank" rel="noopener">newbie in Python</a> or R, you should not be overwhelmed by the presence of available codes in this article.</p>

<h2>What is Naive Bayes algorithm?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">It is a <a href="https://courses.analyticsvidhya.com/courses/introduction-to-data-science-2/?utm_source=blog&amp;utm_medium=6stepsnaivebayesarticle" target="_blank" rel="noopener">classification technique</a> based on <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Bayes&#8217; Theorem</a> with an assumption of independence among predictors. In simple terms, a Naive Bayes classifier assumes that the presence of a particular feature in a class is unrelated to the presence of any other feature. For example, a fruit may be considered to be an apple if it is red, round, and about 3 inches in diameter. Even if these features depend on each other or upon the existence of the other features, all of these properties independently contribute to the probability that this fruit is an apple and that is why it is known as &#8216;Naive&#8217;.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Naive Bayes model is easy to build and particularly useful for very large data sets. Along with simplicity, Naive Bayes is known to outperform even highly sophisticated classification methods.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Bayes theorem provides a way of calculating posterior probability P(c|x) from P(c), P(x) and P(x|c). Look at the equation below:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/Bayes_rule-300x172.png><img class="size-medium wp-image-19916 aligncenter" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/09/Bayes_rule-300x172-300x172.png" alt="Bayes_rule-300x172" width="300" height="172" /></a>Above,</p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><i>P</i>(<i>c|x</i>) is the posterior probability of <i>class</i> (c, <i>target</i>) given <i>predictor</i> (x, <i>attributes</i>).</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><i>P</i>(<i>c</i>) is the prior probability of <i>class</i>.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><i>P</i>(<i>x|c</i>) is the likelihood which is the probability of <i>predictor</i> given <i>class</i>.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><i>P</i>(<i>x</i>) is the prior probability of <i>predictor</i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>How Naive Bayes algorithm works?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Let’s understand it using an example. Below I have a training data set of weather and corresponding target variable ‘Play’ (suggesting possibilities of playing). Now, we need to classify whether players will play or not based on weather condition. Let’s follow the below steps to perform it.</p>
<p>Step 1: Convert the data set into a frequency table</p>
<p>Step 2: Create Likelihood table by finding the probabilities like Overcast probability = 0.29 and probability of playing is 0.64.</p>
<p><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Bayes_41.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18801 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Bayes_41.png" alt="Bayes_4" width="590" height="215" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Bayes_41.png 861w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Bayes_41-300x109.png 300w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Bayes_41-850x310.png 850w" sizes="(max-width: 590px) 100vw, 590px" /></a></p>
<p>Step 3: Now, use Naive Bayesian equation to calculate the posterior probability for each class. The class with the highest posterior probability is the outcome of prediction.</p>
<p><strong>Problem: </strong>Players will play if weather is sunny. Is this statement is correct?</p>
<p>We can solve it using above discussed method of posterior probability.</p>
<p>P(Yes | Sunny) = P( Sunny | Yes) * P(Yes) / P (Sunny)</p>
<p>Here we have P (Sunny |Yes) = 3/9 = 0.33, P(Sunny) = 5/14 = 0.36, P( Yes)= 9/14 = 0.64</p>
<p>Now, P (Yes | Sunny) = 0.33 * 0.64 / 0.36 = 0.60, which has higher probability.</p>
<p>Naive Bayes uses a similar method to predict the probability of different class based on various attributes. This algorithm is mostly used in text classification and with problems having multiple classes.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>What are the Pros and Cons of Naive Bayes?</h2>
<p><em><strong>Pros:</strong></em></p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">It is easy and fast to predict class of test data set. It also perform well in multi class prediction</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">When assumption of independence holds, a Naive Bayes classifier performs better compare to other models like logistic regression and you need less training data.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">It perform well in case of categorical input variables compared to numerical variable(s). For numerical variable, normal distribution is assumed (bell curve, which is a strong assumption).</li>
</ul>
<p><em><strong>Cons:</strong></em></p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">If categorical variable has a category (in test data set), which was not observed in training data set, then model will assign a 0 (zero) probability and will be unable to make a prediction. This is often known as &#8220;Zero Frequency&#8221;. To solve this, we can use the smoothing technique. One of the simplest smoothing techniques is called Laplace estimation.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">On the other side naive Bayes is also known as a bad estimator, so the probability outputs from <tt class="docutils literal"><span class="pre">predict_proba</span></tt> are not to be taken too seriously.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Another limitation of Naive Bayes is the assumption of independent predictors. In real life, it is almost impossible that we get a set of predictors which are completely independent.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>4 Applications of Naive Bayes Algorithms</h2>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Real time Prediction: </strong>Naive Bayes is an eager learning classifier and it is sure fast. Thus, it could be used for making predictions in real time.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Multi class Prediction: </strong>This algorithm is also well known for multi class prediction feature. Here we can predict the probability of multiple classes of target variable.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Text classification/ Spam Filtering/ Sentiment Analysis:</strong> Naive Bayes classifiers mostly used in text classification (due to better result in multi class problems and independence rule) have higher success rate as compared to other algorithms. As a result, it is widely used in Spam filtering (identify spam e-mail) and Sentiment Analysis (in social media analysis, to identify positive and negative customer sentiments)</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Recommendation System: </strong>Naive Bayes Classifier and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collaborative_filtering" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Collaborative Filtering</a> together builds a Recommendation System that uses machine learning and data mining techniques to filter unseen information and predict whether a user would like a given resource or not</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>How to build a basic model using Naive Bayes in Python?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Again, scikit learn (python library) will help here to build a Naive Bayes model in Python. There are three types of Naive Bayes model under scikit learn library:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><a href="http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/naive_bayes.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Gaussian:</a> </strong>It is used in classification and it assumes that features follow a normal distribution.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><a href="http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/naive_bayes.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Multinomial</a>: </strong>It is used for discrete counts. For example, let&#8217;s say,  we have a text classification problem. Here we can consider bernoulli trials which is one step further and instead of &#8220;word occurring in the document&#8221;, we have &#8220;count how often word occurs in the document&#8221;, you can think of it as &#8220;number of times outcome number x_i is observed over the n trials&#8221;.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong><a href="http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/naive_bayes.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Bernoulli</a>: </strong>The binomial model is useful if your feature vectors are binary (i.e. zeros and ones). One application would be text classification with &#8216;bag of words&#8217; model where the 1s &amp; 0s are &#8220;word occurs in the document&#8221; and &#8220;word does not occur in the document&#8221; respectively.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Based on your data set, you can choose any of above discussed model. Below is the example of Gaussian model.</p>
<h3>Python Code</h3>
<pre><span class="kn">#Import Library of Gaussian Naive Bayes model
</span><span class="kn">from</span> <span class="nn">sklearn.naive_bayes</span> <span class="kn">import</span> <span class="n">GaussianNB
import numpy as np

#assigning predictor and target variables
x= </span><span class="n">np</span><span class="o">.</span><span class="n">array</span><span class="p">([[-3,7],[1,5], [1,2], [-2,0], [2,3], [-4,0], [-1,1], [1,1], [-2,2], [2,7], [-4,1], [-2,7]])</span>
<span class="n">Y</span> <span class="o">=</span> <span class="n">np</span><span class="o">.</span><span class="n">array</span><span class="p">([3, 3, 3, 3, 4, 3, 3, 4, 3, 4, 4, 4</span><span class="p">])</span></pre>
<pre><span class="n">#Create a Gaussian Classifier
</span><span class="n">model</span> <span class="o">=</span> <span class="n">GaussianNB</span><span class="p">()
</span><span class="c">
# Train the model using the training sets 
</span><span class="n">model</span><span class="o">.</span><span class="n">fit</span><span class="p">(x</span><span class="p">,</span> <span class="n">y</span><span class="p">)

</span><span class="c">#Predict Output 
predicted= model</span><span class="o">.</span><span class="n">predict</span><span class="p">([[1,2],[3,4]]</span><span class="p">)
print predicted

<strong>Output: ([3,4])
</strong></span></pre>

<pre>require(e1071) #Holds the Naive Bayes Classifier

<p style="text-align: justify;">Above, we looked at the basic Naive Bayes model, you can improve the power of this basic model by tuning parameters and handle assumption intelligently. Let&#8217;s look at the methods to improve the performance of Naive Bayes Model. I&#8217;d recommend you to go through <a href="http://www.inf.ed.ac.uk/teaching/courses/inf2b/learnnotes/inf2b-learn-note07-2up.pdf" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">this document</a> for more details on Text classification using Naive Bayes.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>Tips to improve the power of Naive Bayes Model</h2>
<p>Here are some tips for improving power of Naive Bayes Model:</p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">If continuous features do not have normal distribution, we should use transformation or different methods to convert it in normal distribution.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">If test data set has zero frequency issue, apply smoothing techniques &#8220;Laplace Correction&#8221; to predict the class of test data set.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Remove correlated features, as the highly correlated features are voted twice in the model and it can lead to over inflating importance.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Naive Bayes classifiers has limited options for parameter tuning like alpha=1 for smoothing, fit_prior=[True|False] to learn class prior probabilities or not and some other options (look at detail <a href="http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.naive_bayes.MultinomialNB.html#sklearn.naive_bayes.MultinomialNB" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">here</a>). I would recommend to focus on your  pre-processing of data and the feature selection.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">You might think to apply some <em>classifier combination technique like </em>ensembling, bagging and boosting but these methods would not help. Actually, &#8220;ensembling, boosting, bagging&#8221; won&#8217;t help since their purpose is to reduce variance. Naive Bayes has no variance to minimize.</li>
</ul>

<p>It&#8217;s a trivial example for illustration. The &#8220;Likelihood table&#8221; (a confusing misnomer, I think) is in fact a probability table that has the JOINT weather and play outcome probabilities in the center, and the MARGINAL probabilities of one variable (from integrating out the other variable from the joint) on the side and bottom. </p>
<p>Say, weather type = w and play outcome = p.<br />
P(w,p) is the joint probabilities and P(p) and P(w) are the marginals. Bayes rule described above by Sunil stems from:<br />
P(w,p) = P(w|p) * P(p) = P(p|w) * P(w).<br />
From the center cells we have P(w,p) and from the side/bottom we get P(p) and P(w).<br />
Depending on what you need to calculate, it follows that:<br />
(1): P(w|p) = P(w,p) / P(p) and<br />
(2:)P(p|w) = P(w,p) / P(w), which is what you did with P(sunny,yes) = 3/14 and P(w) = 5/14, yielding (3/14 ) ( 14/5), with the 14&#8217;s cancelling out.</p>
<p>The main Bayes take away is that often, one of the two quantities above, P(w|p) or P(p|w) is much harder to get at than the other. So if you&#8217;re a practitioner you&#8217;ll come to see this as one of two mathematical miracles regarding this topic, the other being the applicability of Markov Chain Monte Carlo in circumventing some nasty integrals Bayes might throw at you. But I digress. </p>

Erdem.</p>

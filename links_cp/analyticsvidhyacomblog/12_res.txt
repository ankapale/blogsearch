<h2 style="text-align: justify;">1. ChennaiÂ Floods &#8211; Quick Story</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In NovemberâDecember 2015, the annual northeast monsoon generated heavy rainfall in the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coromandel_Coast" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Coromandel Coast</a>Â region of the South Indian States of Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh, the union territory of Pondicherry with the city of Chennai particularly hard-hit.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">TermedÂ  <strong>2015 South Indian floods</strong> asÂ the âblack swanâ caused an enormous destruction.Â It is estimated that around 500 people lost their lives and over 18 lakh (1.8 million) people were displaced.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">With approximations of damages and losses ranging from â¹50000 crore (US$7 billion) to â¹100000 crore (US$15 billion), the floods were the costliest to have occurred in 2015, and were amongÂ  the costliest natural disasters of the year. The flooding has been attributed to the <em>El NiÃ±o</em> phenomenon.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In the city of Chennai, the impact of the heavy rainfall was compounded by the overfull water reservoirs, which soon started to spill over into the city. Whole neighborhoods were inundated, with loss of power, communication and access to essentials. The National Disaster Response Force (NDRF), the Indian Army and disaster management units were pressed into service. Rescue and rehabilitation efforts spanned well over 2 weeks.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">What emerged conspicuously during this unprecedented crisis was the coordination of relief efforts over social media platforms such as Twitter, Facebook and WhatsApp. Volunteers, NGOs and other rescue parties were observed to be sending out alerts, requests and sharing useful information related to the flooding on social media. Others used social media to check up on their loved ones, share information, express opinions and sending out requests for help.</p>

<h2 style="text-align: justify;">2. Exploratory Data Analysis</h2>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">2.1Â Â  The typical tweet</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A tweet is a social media message posted on Twitter.com. It is restricted to 140 characters. Though most tweets contain mostly text, it is possible to embed URLs, pictures, videos, vines and GIFs.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Tweets contain components called hashtags, which are words that capture the subject of the tweet. They are prefixed by the â#â character. They can also convey emotion (#sarcasm) or an event (#IndiaVsPakistan) or popular catchphrase in pop culture (#ICantBreathe).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Usernames or handles of those who post are recognized by the â@â symbol. A user can direct a message to another user by adding the handle, with the â@â symbol.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A retweet (ârtâ for short) is a tweet by a user X that has been shared by user Y to all of Yâs followers. Thus, retweets are a way of measuring how popular the tweet is.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A user can âfavoriteâ a tweet; this is analogous to a âLikeâ on Facebook.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">2.2 Â  Data Extraction</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Twitter has an official API called OAuth, a token-based authentication system that indexesÂ  tweets that match a given search string and writes the output to a file. While this service is free and convenient to perform a quick and efficient extraction of tweets, it has a crucial limitation: it can retrieve tweets only from the previous week.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Hence, a repository on the code sharing portal <a href="https://github.com/Jefferson-Henrique/GetOldTweets-python" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">GitHub</a>Â was utilized to search for and extract tweets. The search term was the hashtag <code>#ChennaiFloods</code>. The python language was used (in an Ubuntu Linux environment) to perform the extraction; the extracted tweets were written to a comma-separated value file.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">2.3 Data Preparation &amp; Exploration</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The information gathered contained hashtags and mentions of the users who tweeted about this event. These were separated in R using regular expressions.</p>

<h3 style="text-align: justify;">2.4Â  Â Text Preparation</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The tweets were parsed into a corpus for text analysis. The following steps were executed to clean the corpus and prepare it for further analysis. Only the text portion of the tweet (the actual message) was considered.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Removing numbers: </strong>TweetIDs are number generated by Twitter to identify each tweet. Numbers as such donât serve any purpose for text analysis and hence they are discarded.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Removing URLs &amp; links: </strong>Many tweets contained links to webpages and videos elsewhere on the Internet. These were removed with regular expressions.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Removing stopwords: </strong>Stopwords are words in English that are commonly used in every sentence, but have no analytical significance. Examples are âisâ, âbutâ, âshallâ, âbyâ etc. These words were removed by matching the corpus with the stopwords list in the tm package of R. Expletives were also removed.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Removing non-English words: </strong>The corpus generated after performing the last 3 steps were broken into their constituent words and all English words and words less than 4 characters long were removed. What remained was the list of non-English words, words that mentioned areas in Chennai, words that are actually Tamil words written in English and misspellings of normal English words.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The non-English words that denoted the names of localities were used to form a wordcloud.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><img class="aligncenter wp-image-25805" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/4.jpg" alt="4" width="600" height="367" /></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Stemming words: </strong>In text analysis, stemming is <em>âthe process of reducing inflected (or sometimes derived) words to their word stem, base or root formâgenerally a written word formâ</em>. Stemming is done to reduce inflectional forms and sometimes derivationally related-forms of a word to a common base form. Many methods exist to stem words in a corpus.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Suffix-dropping algorithms:</strong> The last parts of all the words get truncated. For example, words like âprogrammingâ, âprogrammerâ, âprogrammedâ, âprogrammableâ can all be stemmed to the root âprogramâ. On the other hand, ârescuingâ, ârescueâ, ârescuedâ are stemmed to form ârescuâ, which is not a word or a root.Â This method was chosen for this study for simplicity.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Lemmatisation algorithms:</strong> Each word is the determination of the <em>lemma </em>for a word in the corpus. This is done with the understanding of the context, part of speech and the lexicon for the language. For example, âbetterâ is related to âgoodâ, ârunningâ is related to âwalkâ and so on.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>n-gram analysis:</strong> Each word is broken into a part of its whole by ânâ characters, and the one that makes most sense is retained. For example, for n=1 (unigram), the letters âfâ, âlâ, âoâ, âoâ, âdâ are individually parsed from âfloodâ. For a higher n (say n=5), âfloodâ is retained from âfloodingâ, although at n=4, âdingâ can also be construed as a word.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Removing punctuation: </strong>Punctuation marks make no impact to the analysis of text and are hence removed.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Stripping whitespace: </strong>Words that have extra whitespaces at the beginning, middle or end are subjected to a regular expression that removes the whitespace and retains only the words themselves.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Checking for impure characters: </strong>A check on the corpus after the modifications made thus far revealed that some URLs were left behind, due to the removal of whitespaces, numbers and punctuations. Regular expressions were used to remove them.</p>

<h2 style="text-align: justify;">3. Clustering and Topic Modeling</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">When a crisis like the Chennai floods strikes, a large number of similar tweets get generated that it becomes challenging to make meaningful interpretations from the huge volumes of data that need to be processed.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">One solution is to cluster similar tweets together after performing the necessary EDA operations that it becomes easier to manage the flow of information.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">3.1Â Â  Hierarchical Clustering</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Hierarchical clustering attempts to build different levels of clusters. Strategies for hierarchical clustering fall into two types (Wikipedia, n.d.):</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Agglomerative:</strong> where we start out with each document in its own cluster. The algorithm iteratively merges documents or clusters that are closest to each other until the entire corpus forms a single cluster. Each merge happens at a different (increasing) distance.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><strong>Divisive:</strong> where we start out with the entire set of documents in a single cluster. At each step the algorithm splits the cluster recursively until each document is in its own cluster. This is basically the inverse of an agglomerative strategy.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The results of hierarchical clustering are usually presented inÂ a dendrogram.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The R function, hclust() was used to perform hierarchical clustering. It uses the agglomerative method. The following steps explain hierarchical clustering in simple terms:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Assign each document to its own (single member) cluster</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Find the pair of clusters that are closest to each other and merge them, leaving us with one less cluster</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Compute distances between the new cluster and each of the old clusters</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Repeat steps 2 and 3 until you have a single cluster containing all documents</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">To perform this operation, the corpus was converted into a matrix with each tweet (or âdocumentâ) given an ID. Extremely sparse rows, i.e. rows with elements that are part of less than 2% of the entire corpus were removed. <strong>Wardâs method for hierarchical clustering </strong>was used.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The dendrogram output is to be interpreted as follows:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Farther the nodes, greater is the dissimilarity and more robust is that</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The closer the node, the weaker is the</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The height of each node in the plot is proportional to the value of the intergroup dissimilarity between its two</p>

<h3 style="text-align: justify;">3.2Â Â  K-Means Clustering</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">As opposed hierarchical clustering, where one does not arrive at the number of clusters until after the dendrogram, in K-means, the number of clusters is decided beforehand. The algorithm then generates k document clusters in a way that ensures the within-cluster distances from eachÂ  cluster member to the centroid (or geometric mean) of the cluster is minimized.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A simplified description of the algorithm is as follows:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Assign the documents randomly to k bins</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Compute the location of the centroid of each</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Compute the distance between each document and each centroid</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Assign each document to the bin corresponding to the centroid closest to</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Stop if no document is moved to a new bin, else go to step</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">3.2.1 Choosing k</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The most significant factor of employing k-means clustering is choosing the no. of clusters, âkâ. The â<em>elbow methodâ</em>, wherein the SUM of Squared Error (SSE, the sum of the squared distance between each member of the cluster and its centroid) decreases abruptly at that value that is theoretically the optimal value of k, is widely applied to arrive at k.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">When k is plotted against the SSE, it will be seen that the error decreases as k gets larger; this is because when the number of clusters increases, they become smaller, and hence the distortion is also smaller.</p>

<h3 style="text-align: justify;">3.3 Topic Modeling</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Another technique that is employed to deduce the themes of text is topic modeling.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A topic model is a type of statistical model for discovering the abstract &#8220;topics&#8221; that occur in a collection of documents (tweets in this case). Intuitively, given that a document is about a particular topic, one would expect particular words to appear in the document more or less frequently: in this case, âhelpâ is quite common to almost every tweet.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">A document typically concerns multiple topics in different proportions; thus, in a document that is 10% about subject A and 90% about subject B, there would probably be about 9 times more words about âBâ than words about âAâ (Wikipedia, n.d.).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Topic modeling has implementations in various algorithms, but the most common algorithm in use is Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA).</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">3.3.1 Latent Dirichlet Allocation</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a statistical model that allows sets of observations to be explained by unobserved groups that explain why some parts of the data are similar. For example, if observations are words collected into documents, it posits that each document is a mixture of a small number of topics and that each word&#8217;s creation is attributable to one of the document&#8217;s topics.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">LDA allows the possibility of a document to arise from a combination of topics. For example, the following tweet may be classified as (say) 90% information &amp; 10% sympathy/hope.</p>

<h3 style="text-align: justify;">4.2 Â  Challenges to Real-Time Analysis of Tweets</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The following points highlight a few challenges that will be faced by any researcher trying to solve the same problem.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Retweets contain information that many users find relevant. The subjectivity of this relevance to the crisis at hand is difficult, if not impossible to measure.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">This problem is compounded if the tweets contain no hashtags. In the dataset generated for this analysis, 1399 tweets (22%) had no hashtags. These tweets may also be highly relevant to the crisis but may be missed due to the lack of hashtags.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Twitter has a 140 character-limit on all tweets (not including pictures and videos). This leads users to truncate or shorten words to forms that is easily interpretable to humans, but is challenging for a machine. Eg: âafcted pplâ is easily understandable to mean âaffected peopleâ for a human, but not for a program. One way to solve this problem is to maintain a dictionary of such terms and match them in real-time.</p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;">4.3 Â  Applications &amp; Scope for Further Work</h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;">As mentioned in the introductory chapter, this is an active field. The power of social media will continue to be researched and newer applications will continue to be built to harness itsÂ  power.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">One area is quashing rumors. During the Chennai floods, quite a number of false ânews reportsâ and âalertsâ circulated on Facebook, Twitter and the mobile messaging application WhatsApp. Machine learning can be employed to check the veracity of social media by comparing contents from actual news reports and</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">During the 2011 Australia floods, the civic authorities were among the most prolific users of Twitter in disseminating safety tips, general information and coordinating volunteer relief work. Every civic authority would do well to develop a framework and system to manage crises also through social media. This covers all disasters, both natural (earthquakes, floods, hurricanes) and man-made (terror strikes, shootouts).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Media outlets and government agencies can work together in planning for incidents that are expected by creating distinct identifiers and hashtags for each scenario and making the public aware of the</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Disasters may strike at any time. While it may not be possible to prevent them, it is prudent to be prepared for unfortunate eventualities. Having a dedicated social network analysis platform to analyze information in real-time will definitely aid in this endeavor.</p>

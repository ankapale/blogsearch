<h2>What is Regression Analysis?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Regression analysis is a form of predictive modelling technique which investigates the relationship between a <strong>dependentÂ </strong>(target)Â and <strong>independent variable (s)</strong> (predictor).Â This technique is used for forecasting, time series modelling and finding the <a href="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2015/06/establish-causality-events/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">causal effect relationship</a> between the variables. For example, relationship between rash driving andÂ number of road accidents by a driver is best studied through regression.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Regression analysis is an important tool for modelling and analyzing data.Â Here, we fit a curve / line to the data points, in such a manner that the differences betweenÂ the distances of data points from the curve or line is minimized. Â I&#8217;ll explain this in more details in coming sections.<a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Line.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18935 size-full" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Line.png" alt="Regression_Line" width="689" height="283" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Line.png 689w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Line-300x123.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 689px) 100vw, 689px" /></a></p>

<h2>Why do we use Regression Analysis?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">As mentioned above, regression analysis estimatesÂ the relationship between two or more variables. Let&#8217;s understand this withÂ an easy example:</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Let&#8217;s say, you want to estimate growth in sales of a company based on current economic conditions. You have the recent company data whichÂ indicates that the growth in sales is around two and a half times the growth in the economy. Using this insight, we can predict future sales of the company based on current &amp; past information.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">There are multiple benefits of using regression analysis. They are as follows:</p>
<ol>
<li>It indicatesÂ theÂ <strong>significant relationships</strong> between dependent variable and independent variable.</li>
<li>It indicates theÂ <strong>strength of impact</strong> of multipleÂ independent variables on a dependent variable.</li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Regression analysis also allows us to compare the effects of variables measured on different scales, such as the effect of price changes and the number of promotional activities.Â These benefits help market researchers / data analysts / data scientists to eliminate and evaluateÂ the best set of variables to be used forÂ building predictive models.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>How many types of regression techniquesÂ do we have?</h2>
<p>There are various kinds of regression techniques available to make predictions. These techniques are mostly driven byÂ three metrics (number of independent variables, type of dependent variables and shape of regression line). We&#8217;ll discuss them inÂ detail in the following sections.</p>
<p><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Type.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18917 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Type.png" alt="Regression_Type" width="609" height="234" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Type.png 775w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Regression_Type-300x115.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 609px) 100vw, 609px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">For the creative ones, you can even cook up new regressions, if you feel the need to use a combination of the parameters above, which people haven&#8217;t used before. But before you start that, let us understand the most commonly used regressions:</p>

<h2>1. Linear Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">It is one of the most widely known modeling technique. Linear regression is usually among the first few topics which people pick while learning predictive modeling.Â In this technique,Â the dependent variable is continuous, independent variable(s) can be <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_and_discrete_variables" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">continuous or discrete</a>,Â and nature of regression line is linear.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Linear RegressionÂ establishes aÂ relationship between <strong>dependent variable (Y)</strong> and one or more <strong>independentÂ variables (X)</strong> using a <strong>best fit straight line</strong> (also known as regression line).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">It isÂ represented by an equation <strong>Y=a+b*X + e</strong>, where a is intercept, b is slope of the lineÂ andÂ e is error term. This equation can be used to predict the value of target variable based on given predictorÂ variable(s).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Linear_Regression1.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18912 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Linear_Regression1.png" alt="Linear_Regression" width="378" height="225" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Linear_Regression1.png 476w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Linear_Regression1-300x178.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 378px) 100vw, 378px" /></a></p>
<p>The difference between simple linear regression and multiple linear regression is that, multiple linear regression has (&gt;1) independent variables, whereas simple linear regression has only 1 independent variable. Â Now, the question is &#8220;How do we obtainÂ best fit line?&#8221;.</p>
<h4>How toÂ obtain best fit line (Value of a and b)?</h4>
<p style="text-align: justify;">This task can be easily accomplished by Least Square Method.Â It isÂ the most common method used for fitting a regression line. ItÂ calculates the best-fit line for the observed data by minimizing the sum of the squares of the vertical deviations from each data point to the line. Because the deviations are first squared, when added, thereÂ isÂ no cancelling out between positive and negative values.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Least_Square.png><img class=" size-full wp-image-18938 aligncenter" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Least_Square.png" alt="Least_Square" width="191" height="57" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/reg_error.gif><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18916" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/reg_error.gif" alt="reg_error" width="296" height="317" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">We can evaluateÂ the model performance using the metricÂ <strong>R-square</strong>. To know more details about these metrics, you can read:Â Model Performance metrics <a href="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2015/01/model-performance-metrics-classification/">Part 1</a>, <a href="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2015/01/model-perform-part-2/">Part 2</a>Â .</p>
<h4>Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">There must be <strong>linear relationship</strong> between independent and dependent variables</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Multiple regression suffers from <strong>multicollinearity, autocorrelation, heteroskedasticity</strong>.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Linear Regression is very sensitive to <strong>Outliers</strong>. It can terribly affect the regression line andÂ eventually the forecasted values.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Multicollinearity can increase the variance of the coefficient estimates and make the estimates very sensitive to minor changes in the model. The result is that the coefficient estimates are unstable</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">In case of multiple independent variables, we can go with <strong>forward selection</strong>, <strong>backward elimination</strong> and <strong>step wise approach</strong> for selection of most significant independent variables.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>2. Logistic Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Logistic regression is used to find the probability of event=Success and event=Failure. We shouldÂ use logistic regression when theÂ dependent variable is binary (0/ 1, True/ False, Yes/ No) in nature. Here the value of Y ranges from 0 to 1 and it can represented by following equation.</p>
<pre>odds= p/ (1-p) = probability of event occurrence /Â probability of not event occurrence
ln(odds) = ln(p/(1-p))
logit(p) = ln(p/(1-p)) = b0+b1X1+b2X2+b3X3....+bkXk</pre>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Above, p is the probability of presence of the characteristic of interest. A question that you should ask here isÂ &#8220;why have we used log in the equation?&#8221;.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">SinceÂ we are working here with aÂ binomial distribution (dependent variable), we need to choose a link function which is best suited for thisÂ distribution. And, it is <b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_function" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">logit</a></b> function. In the equation above, the parameters are chosenÂ to maximize the likelihood of observing the sample values rather than minimizing the sum of squared errors (like in ordinary regression).</p>
<p><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Logistic_Regression.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18792 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Logistic_Regression.png" alt="Logistic_Regression" width="359" height="274" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Logistic_Regression.png 533w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Logistic_Regression-300x229.png 300w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Logistic_Regression-83x63.png 83w" sizes="(max-width: 359px) 100vw, 359px" /></a></p>
<h4>Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">It is widely used for <strong>classification problems</strong></li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">LogisticÂ regression doesn&#8217;t requireÂ linear relationship between dependent and independent variables.Â  ItÂ can handle various types of relationships because it applies a non-linear log transformation to the predicted odds ratio</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">To avoidÂ over fitting and under fitting, we should includeÂ all significant variables. A good approach to ensure this practice is to use a step wise method to estimate the logistic regression</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">It requires <strong>large sample sizes</strong> because maximum likelihood estimates are less powerful at low sample sizes than ordinary least square</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">The independent variables should not be correlated with each otherÂ i.e. <strong>no multi collinearity</strong>.Â  However, we have the options to include interaction effects of categorical variables in the analysis and in the model.</li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">If theÂ values of dependent variable is ordinal, then it is called as <strong>Ordinal logistic regression</strong></li>
<li style="text-align: justify;">If dependent variable is multi class then it is known as <strong>Multinomial Logistic regression</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>3. PolynomialÂ Regression</h2>
<p>A regression equation is a polynomial regression equation if the power of independent variable is more than 1. The equation below represents a polynomial equation:</p>
<pre style="text-align: center;">y=a+b*x^2</pre>
<p>In this regression technique, the best fit line is not a straight line. ItÂ is rather a curve that fits into the data points.</p>
<p><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Polynomial.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18932 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Polynomial.png" alt="Polynomial" width="357" height="216" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Polynomial.png 482w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Polynomial-300x182.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 357px) 100vw, 357px" /></a></p>
<h4>Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">While there might be a temptation to fit a higher degree polynomial to get lower error, this can result in over-fitting. Always plot the relationships to see the fit and focus on making sure that the curve fits the nature of the problem. Here is an example of how plotting can help:</li>
</ul>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/underfitting-overfitting.png><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-13056" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/underfitting-overfitting.png" alt="underfitting-overfitting" width="555" height="140" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/underfitting-overfitting.png 555w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/underfitting-overfitting-300x76.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 555px) 100vw, 555px" /></a></p>
<ul>
<li style="text-align: justify;">Especially look out for curve towards the ends and see whether those shapes and trends make sense. Higher polynomials can end up producing wierd results on extrapolation.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>4. StepwiseÂ Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">This form of regression is usedÂ when we deal with multiple independent variables. In this technique, the selection of independent variables isÂ done with the help of an automatic process, which involvesÂ <em>no</em> human intervention.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">This featÂ is achieved by observingÂ statisticalÂ values like R-square, t-stats and AIC metric to discernÂ significant variables.Â Stepwise regression basically fits the regression model by adding/dropping co-variates one at a time based on a specified criterion. Some of the most commonly used Stepwise regression methods are listed below:</p>
<ul class="ul">
<li class="li">Standard stepwise regression does two things. ItÂ adds and removes predictors as needed for each step.</li>
<li class="li">Forward selection starts with most significant predictor in the model and adds variable for each step.</li>
<li class="li">Backward elimination starts with all predictors in the model and removes the least significant variable for each step.</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify;">The aim of this modeling technique is to maximize the prediction power with minimum number of predictor variables.Â It is one of the method to handle<a href="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2015/07/dimension-reduction-methods/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"> higher dimensionality</a> of data set.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>5. RidgeÂ Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Ridge Regression is a technique used when the data suffers from multicollinearity ( independent variables are highly correlated). InÂ multicollinearity, even though the least squares estimates (OLS) are unbiased, their variances are large which deviates the observed value farÂ from the true value. By adding a degree of bias to the regression estimates, ridge regression reduces the standard errors.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Above, we sawÂ the equation for linear regression.Â Remember? It can be represented as:</p>
<p style="text-align: left;">y=a+ b*x</p>
<p style="text-align: left;">This equation also has an error term. The complete equation becomes:</p>
<pre style="text-align: left;">y=a+b*x+e (error term),Â  [error term is the value needed to correct for a prediction error between the observed and predicted value]</pre>
<pre style="text-align: left;">=&gt; y=a+y= a+ b1x1+ b2x2+....+e, for multiple independent variables.</pre>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In a linear equation, prediction errors can be decomposed into two sub components. First isÂ due to the <strong>biased</strong> and second isÂ due to theÂ <strong>variance</strong>.Â Prediction error can occur due to any one of these two orÂ both components. Here, we&#8217;ll discuss about the error caused due to variance.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Ridge regression solves the multicollinearity problem throughÂ <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shrinkage_estimator" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">shrinkage parameter</a> Î» (lambda). Look at the equation below.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Ridge2.png><img class="aligncenter wp-image-18925 " src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Ridge2.png" alt="Ridge" width="321" height="114" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Ridge2.png 414w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Ridge2-300x107.png 300w" sizes="(max-width: 321px) 100vw, 321px" /></a></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">In this equation, we have two components. First one is least square term and other one is lambda of the summation ofÂ <span id="MathJax-Span-27" class="mi">Î²</span><span id="MathJax-Span-28" class="mn">2 (beta- square) whereÂ Î² is the coefficient. This is added to least square term in order to shrink the parameter to have a very low variance.</span></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify;">Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li>The assumptions of this regression is same as least squared regression except normality is not to beÂ assumed</li>
<li>It shrinks the value of coefficients but doesn&#8217;t reachesÂ zero, which suggests no feature selection feature</li>
<li>This is a regularization method and uses <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regularization_(mathematics)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">l2 regularization</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>6. LassoÂ Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Similar to Ridge Regression, Lasso (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) also penalizes the absolute size of the regression coefficients. In addition, it is capable of reducing the variability and improving the accuracy of linear regression models. Â Look at the equation below:Â <a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Lasso.png><img class=" size-medium wp-image-18926 aligncenter" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Lasso-300x107.png" alt="Lasso" width="300" height="107" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Lasso-300x107.png 300w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Lasso.png 414w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a>Lasso regression differs from ridge regression in a way thatÂ it usesÂ absolute values in the penalty function, instead of squares. This leads toÂ penalizing (or equivalently constraining the sum of the absolute values of the estimates) valuesÂ which causesÂ some of the parameter estimates to turn out exactly zero. Larger the penalty applied, further the estimates getÂ shrunk towards absolute zero. ThisÂ results to variable selection out of given n variables.</p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify;">Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li>The assumptions of this regression is same as least squared regression except normality is not to beÂ assumed</li>
<li>It shrinks coefficients to zero (exactly zero), which certainly helps in feature selection</li>
<li>This is a regularization method and uses <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regularization_(mathematics)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">l1 regularization</a></li>
<li>If group of predictors are highly correlated, lasso picks only one of them and shrinks the others to zero</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2>7.Â ElasticNet Regression</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">ElasticNet is hybrid of Lasso and Ridge Regression techniques.Â <span class="pre">It</span>Â is trained with L1 and L2 prior as regularizer.Â Elastic-net is useful when there are multiple features which are correlated. Lasso is likely to pick one of these at random, while elastic-net is likely to pick both.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><a rel="lightbox" href=https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Elastic_Net.png><img class=" size-medium wp-image-18928 aligncenter" src="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Elastic_Net-300x31.png" alt="Elastic_Net" width="300" height="31" srcset="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Elastic_Net-300x31.png 300w, https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Elastic_Net.png 376w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" /></a></p>
<p>A practical advantage of trading-off between Lasso and Ridge is that, it allows Elastic-Net to inherit some of Ridgeâs stability under rotation.</p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify;">Important Points:</h4>
<ul>
<li>It encourages group effect in case of highly correlated variables</li>
<li>There are no limitations on the number of selected variables</li>
<li>It can suffer with double shrinkage</li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Beyond these 7Â most commonly used regression techniques, you can also look at other models like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayesian_linear_regression" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Bayesian</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_regression" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Ecological</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robust_regression" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Robust regression</a>.</p>

<h2>How to select the right regression model?</h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;">Life is usually simple, when you know only one or two techniques. One of the training institutes I know of tells their students &#8211; if the outcome is continuous &#8211; apply linear regression. If it is binary &#8211; use logistic regression! However, higher the number of options available at our disposal, more difficult it becomes to choose the right one. A similar case happens with regression models.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">WithinÂ multiple typesÂ of regression models, it is important to choose the best suited techniqueÂ based on type of independent and dependent variables, dimensionality in the data and other essential characteristics of the data. Below are the key factors that you should practice to select theÂ rightÂ regression model:</p>
<ol>
<li>Data exploration is an inevitable part of building predictive model. It should be youÂ first step before selecting the right model like identify the relationship and impact of variables</li>
<li>To compare the goodness of fit for different models, we can analyse different metrics like statistical significance of parameters, R-square, Adjusted r-square, AIC, BIC and error term.Â Another one is the <a href="http://support.minitab.com/en-us/minitab/17/topic-library/modeling-statistics/regression-and-correlation/goodness-of-fit-statistics/what-is-mallows-cp/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer">Mallow&#8217;s Cp</a> criterion. This essentially checks for possible bias in your model, by comparing the model with all possible submodels (or a careful selection of them).</li>
<li>Cross-validation is the best way to evaluate models used for prediction. Here you divide your data set into two group (train and validate).Â A simple mean squared difference between the observed and predicted values give you a measure for the prediction accuracy.</li>
<li>If your data set has multipleÂ confounding variables, you should not chooseÂ automatic model selection methodÂ because you do not want to put theseÂ in a model at the same time.</li>
<li>It&#8217;ll also depend on your objective. It can occur that a less powerful model is easy to implement as compared to a highlyÂ statistically significant model.</li>
<li>Regression regularization methods(Lasso, Ridge and ElasticNet) works well in case of high dimensionality and multicollinearity among the variables in the data set.</li>
</ol>

<p>This is an awesome article. I just want to point out something important for people that may be new to modeling as many data science students I&#8217;ve mentored get overwhelmed and confused about what the different types of regression truly are, and what the true definition of linear regression is.</p>
<p>These aren&#8217;t really different types of regression models per se. This is a mix of different techniques with different characteristics, all of which can be used for linear regression, logistic regression or any other kind of generalized linear model.</p>
<p>Linear and logistic are the only two types of base models covered.</p>
<p>Polynomial is just using transformations of the variables, but the model is still linear in the beta parameters. Thus it is still linear regression. Even a weird model like y = exp(a + bx) is a generalized linear model if we use the log-link for logistic regression. This yields log y = a + bx. This is a concept that bewilders a lot of people.</p>
<p>Stepwise is just a method of building a model by adding and removing variables based on the F statistic.</p>
<p>Ridge, Lasso and ElasticNet aren&#8217;t really regressions, they&#8217;re penalties (regularization) on the loss function (OLS), or log-likelihood function (logistic and GLM). Hence, they are useful for other models that are distinct from regression, like SVMs. We usually say &#8220;linear regression with a regularization term/penalty.&#8221;</p>
<p>To be technical, different regression models would be plain linear, logistic, multinomial, poisson, gamma, Cox, etc.</p>
